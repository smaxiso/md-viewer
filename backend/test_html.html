BuySmart: Unified Forecasting & Buying Strategy

A Synthesis of State-of-the-Art Operations Research & Deep Learning for Cocoblu

Executive Summary

Optimizing inventory for the "long tail" requires a tightly integrated pipeline between Demand Forecasting and Inventory Replenishment. This document outlines the evolution of our entire supply chain architecture across four phases: the current manual baseline, the theoretical textbook foundations, state-of-the-art (SOTA) AI techniques, and a concrete implementation pipeline tailored for Cocoblu.

1. Current Manual Process & V0 Baseline

Currently, procurement for tail items relies heavily on manual buyer intuition. Note: The nuances, constraints, and implicit heuristics of this manual process must be fully audited and understood before any SOTA architecture can be successfully deployed.

To date, our V0 automated implementations have relied on static heuristics and flawed cluster mapping: * V0 Replenishment (Heuristic Buying Plan): The current BigQuery pipeline relies on a deterministic Blended DRR (0.4 * Recent DRR + 0.6 * Lifetime DRR). This static calculation assumes past average demand continues linearly, utterly failing to handle seasonality or promotional spikes. Furthermore, it uses an arbitrary doc_goal (Days of Coverage) instead of explicit safety stock calculations, leading to severe overstocking of stable items and understocking of volatile tail items. Supplier lead times are treated as static constants, ignoring real-world delivery uncertainty. * V0 Forecasting (Clustered Tournament): The current forecasting engine samples ~10 SKUs from 4 distinct clusters, runs a tournament of classic models (Naive, Exponential Smoothing, AutoARIMA, Prophet, DeepAR, etc.), and applies the winning model to the entire cluster. This mapping is heavily suboptimal because it assumes uniform behavior across a cluster, forcing poorly fitting models onto unique SKUs. Additionally, predicting SKUs independently creates hierarchical incoherence—the sum of child ASIN forecasts does not match parent SKU or brand-level forecasts, causing conflicting procurement decisions.

2. The Breakdown of Traditional Baselines (Textbook Models)

Traditional operations research models are fundamentally misaligned with the reality of long-tail retail dynamics: * Economic Order Quantity (EOQ): EOQ assumes the demand rate is constant, continuous, and known with certainty. For tail items experiencing bursty, intermittent demand, constant-rate assumptions are mathematically invalid and lead to suboptimal, continuous drip-ordering that routinely violates vendor Minimum Order Quantities (MOQs). * Min-Max / (s, Q) Continuous Review Systems: These models establish safety stocks using standard deviation multipliers ($z \cdot \sigma$) assuming that demand follows a normal (Gaussian) distribution. Applying normal distributions to zero-inflated, sparse tail data dictates either fractional daily requirements or massive, capital-draining safety stocks to cover "artificial" variance.

The "Sawtooth" Curve (Periodic Review System)

The classic textbook inventory replenishment cycle follows a "sawtooth" pattern under a Periodic Review System (P System). Unlike EOQ where order quantities are fixed, the P System operates on a fixed time interval ($P$) between reviews with a variable order quantity ($Q$). The governing mathematics for the sawtooth curve are as follows: * Target Inventory Level ($T$): The peak inventory position must cover the expected demand ($d$) during the protection interval ($P + L$) plus safety stock. $T = d(P + L) + \text{Safety stock}$ * Order Quantity ($Q$): The replenishment order is the difference between the target inventory level and the current inventory position ($IP$). $Q = T - IP$ * Safety Stock: Must cover demand uncertainty over the entire protection interval, calculated as $z\sigma_{P+L}$, where $z$ is the standard deviation multiplier for the cycle-service level. * Total P System Cost ($C$): Combines holding costs ($H$), ordering costs ($S$), and safety stock holding costs. $C = \frac{dP}{2}(H) + \frac{D}{dP}(S) + H(\text{Safety stock})$

3. The SOTA Paradigm Shift

To effectively manage the tail, Cocoblu must shift to probabilistic, generative, and stochastic optimization architectures. The team's raw proposals have been mathematically corrected here to prevent catastrophic failure at production scale.

  • Mamba-Encoded Multi-Stage ISSM (Corrected Forecasting): The raw proposal suggested using Mamba end-to-end. If applied directly to zero-inflated sparse data with standard loss functions, Mamba will simply predict continuous fractional means, destroying the bursty signal required for tail inventory. Instead, Mamba must act strictly as the temporal feature encoder feeding into a Multi-Stage Innovation State Space Model (ISSM). Stage 0 computes the probability of zero sales, Stage 1 computes exactly one sale, and Stage 2 utilizes a Poisson count regression. Crucially, Stage 2 must use a twice logistic transfer function ($\lambda(y) = g(y)(1 + \kappa g(y))$) instead of an exponential one to prevent catastrophic cancellation errors during heavy-tailed demand spikes.
  • End-to-End Neural Reconciliation (NHR-TFNet): The proposal's suggestion to use Robust MinT for hierarchical reconciliation is computationally intractable. For a 10,000+ node hierarchy, Robust MinT requires inverting a dense covariance matrix, introducing an $O(N^3)$ computational bottleneck. We must swap this to NHR-TFNet, which learns the non-linear hierarchical aggregation matrix during the training phase. At runtime, reconciling 10,000 nodes requires only a fast forward-pass matrix multiplication.
  • Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) via Differentiable Simulators: Rather than relying on parametric assumptions, SOTA supply chain architectures formulate procurement as an exogenous Interactive Decision Process. By converting historical data into a differentiable simulator (DirectBackprop), DRL agents can natively learn policies that account for non-linear state evolutions such as lost sales, correlated demand, and price matching.
  • Generative Quantity-Over-Time (Gen-QOT) Arrivals: We must abandon the assumption that an order of $X$ units arrives precisely $L$ days later. Gen-QOT models utilize an autoregressive deep generative architecture to simulate arbitrary arrival dynamics, accounting for vendors delivering single orders across multiple staggered shipments, stochastic partial fill rates (yields), and downstream MOQ rounding distortions.

4. Actionable Pipeline (What Cocoblu Should Do)

This is the prioritized, mathematically sound technical implementation roadmap for Cocoblu:

  1. Un-Censor Demand via Proxy Signals (Glance Views): The forecasting engine will fail if it trains on raw historical sales, as it will interpret out-of-stock (OOS) days as zero demand. We must calculate the historical conversion rate of web page "glance views" to project counterfactual lost sales during OOS periods, creating an "Availability Corrected Demand" feature.
  2. Deploy Mamba-Encoded ISSM: Implement Mamba strictly as the feature encoder for a 3-Stage Bayesian Likelihood Forecaster. Ensure the twice logistic transfer function is utilized for the Poisson stage to robustify the Newton-Raphson solver against extreme tail spikes.
  3. Implement NHR-TFNet for Coherence: Apply End-to-End Neural Reconciliation to the Mamba-ISSM base forecasts. This will inject macro brand-level trends down to sparse SKU-level forecasts without causing $O(N^3)$ matrix inversion bottlenecks during the daily batch run.
  4. Gen-QOT Vendor Simulation: Discard deterministic $(\mu_L, \sigma_L)$ lead-time assumptions. Train an autoregressive Gen-QOT model on historical vendor delivery data to simulate arbitrary partial fills, staggered shipments, and MOQ constraints.
  5. Sample Average Approximation (SAA) for Replenishment: Explicitly discard Ritchi's formula ($\sigma^2_{LTD} = \mu_L \sigma^2_D + \mu^2_D \sigma^2_L$). It strictly assumes demand and lead times are Gaussian and independent, which completely contradicts the asymmetric, heavy-tailed quantile distributions generated by Mamba. Instead, feed the discrete probabilistic scenarios generated by the ISSM and Gen-QOT directly into a Lower Confidence Bound SAA (LCB-SAA) optimizer. This will maximize expected profit across the empirical distribution while forbidding the solver from stocking "cannibalizing products" that artificially game margins.
  6. Risk-Pooling via Multi-Echelon Centralization: The statistical risk-pooling effect is mathematically diluted if highly volatile tail items are pushed to edge fulfillment centers. Update network Dynamic Programming (DP) routing rules to aggregate tail items at upstream, centralized hubs.
  7. Algorithmic "Zero-Touch" Dropship Transitioning: The ultimate optimization for extreme tail items is removing capital risk entirely. Deploy a dynamic thresholding algorithm: if an item's predicted holding cost over 90 days mathematically exceeds its expected gross margin contribution, autonomously flag the SKU to pivot to a Vendor Drop-Ship (JIT) model.
  8. Implement Dynamic Margin & Multi-Criteria Risk Guardrails: While the Lower Confidence Bound SAA (LCB-SAA) formulation optimizes expected profit across stochastic scenarios, it lacks real-world business context. Replace the V0 static "Red Flag" file with a dynamic multi-criteria risk score that tracks real-time margin decay, historical return rates, quality complaints, and demand stability. If a SKU's margin drops below a threshold, the system must automatically throttle the optimizer's upper bounds or route the SKU into a human-in-the-loop "review pipeline".
  9. Decouple System Architecture for Batch Scalability: Running the heavy probabilistic ML models (Mamba-ISSM) synchronously with the SAA replenishment optimizer will cause catastrophic timeouts during the daily batch run. The Mamba-ISSM forecasting must run asynchronously on a dedicated GPU cluster (or distributed Ray workers), pre-computing and caching the discrete demand scenarios. The stochastic replenishment math (LCB-SAA) must then instantly ingest these pre-computed scenarios inside a highly parallelized execution layer (e.g., BigQuery vector operations or a Ray Plasma object store).
  10. API Fallback & Synchronous Drift Circuit Breakers: Decoupling the forecasting into an asynchronous cache introduces a data-staleness vulnerability. We must implement a lightweight Synchronous Drift Monitor: before the SAA engine executes, compare the trailing 24-hour actuals against the mean of the cached ISSM scenarios. If the deviation breaches a statistical threshold (e.g., >2 standard deviations), the pipeline must invalidate the cache and trigger a synchronous API fallback calculation to re-run the Mamba-ISSM forecast before generating the PO.
